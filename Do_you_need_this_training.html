Identify the Root Cause

Not every performance problem is a training problem. Before jumping to solutions, you need to understand why the gap exists.

Click each potential root cause to explore its implications:

Skill gap? This is the one case where training might actually help. If people genuinely don't know how to do something, structured learning can close that gap.
Process issue? Training won't help here. If the process is broken or unclear, even skilled people will fail. Fix the workflow first.
Motivation gap? This points to leadership, culture, or recognition issues — not a skills deficit. Training rarely increases motivation.
Tools problem? You can train people endlessly, but if they're working with inadequate tools, performance will remain limited. Address the environment.

Talk to the Right People

Your perspective as a manager is partial. Before deciding on any solution, gather insights from the right sources. Who would you talk to first?

Great start. Your team members experience the problem daily. They'll surface friction you've never seen — tool limitations, unclear priorities, missing information. Their insights are essential.
Smart move. Experienced colleagues bring outside perspective and pattern recognition. They've likely seen similar situations and can challenge your assumptions before you commit to a solution.
Proceed carefully. Data confirms what happened, but it rarely tells you why. Reports are a starting point — not a substitute for direct conversation. You risk diagnosing from symptoms alone.

Section 4 of 5

Explore Non-Training Solutions

Training is one tool in a large toolkit. Before reaching for it, consider whether these solutions might work better. Click each to find out how.

⚙️
Improve the process
Redesigning workflows removes friction and ambiguity. People often underperform not because they lack skill, but because the process makes it hard to succeed.
🖥
Provide better tools
Outdated or inadequate tools create ceiling effects on performance. Upgrading systems often yields faster results than months of training.
📋
Clarify expectations
Many performance problems stem from unclear standards. When people don't know exactly what "good" looks like, they can't consistently deliver it.
🔁
Improve feedback loops
Regular, specific feedback enables continuous improvement. Without it, people can't course-correct — no matter how well trained they are.
✅
Section 5 of 5

When Training Is the Right Choice

Training is the right answer — but only in specific situations. For each scenario below, decide whether training is needed.

📱 A new CRM system has been rolled out. The team has never used it before and can't navigate basic features.

✅ Correct. A genuine skill gap with a new system is a classic case for training. People can't perform without foundational knowledge.
The team literally doesn't know how to use the new system. This is a knowledge gap — training is the right solution here.

😔 Employees are disengaged and producing work below their usual standard. Morale seems low across the team.

✅ Correct. Disengagement is a motivation and culture issue — not a skill gap. Training won't address the underlying cause.
Disengagement is rarely solved by training. You'd need to explore what's affecting morale: recognition, workload, management style, or unclear purpose.

📞 A new compliance regulation requires staff to handle customer data differently. The risk is significant.

✅ Correct. New compliance requirements represent a clear knowledge gap with real risk. Structured training is both appropriate and necessary.
Compliance changes require people to learn new behaviours and understand the stakes. This is a genuine use case for training — especially with regulatory risk involved.
